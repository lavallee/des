A synthesis of verified claims from barnowl — where the consensus holds, where it fractures, and what the gaps reveal.

Marc Lavallee · April 24, 2026 · barnowl v2.1 · 847 claims

Editorial Independence

New York — April 24, 2026

Editorial decisions must remain with human journalists. AI systems may surface, rank, or summarize — but publication decisions, editorial framing, and source selection require human sign-off.

This principle appears in 73% of reviewed policies, making it the single most widely-held norm in the dataset. Implementation varies significantly: 42% of organizations have formal sign-off workflows; the remainder rely on cultural norms alone.

Sign-off Any documented human review step that must occur before AI-assisted content is published. Oral approval not counted.

This gap is most pronounced among organizations with fewer than 50 editorial staff, where the overhead of formal processes can exceed the perceived benefit. Smaller organizations disproportionately rely on cultural norms — an approach that correlates with lower audit-trail compliance in the dataset.†

1Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2025 found similar rates across European newsrooms.

The consent gap

Among organizations with sign-off requirements, consent documentation is inconsistent. Only 18% maintain auditable records of AI-assisted editorial decisions1 — a figure that likely understates the problem, since organizations without formal policies have no mechanism for tracking.

"The algorithm suggests; the editor decides." This phrase, or close variants, appeared verbatim in three independent policy documents from newsrooms that had not coordinated on language.

barnowl claim cluster C-2847, synthesis of recent policies, April 2026

The convergence on similar language suggests emerging professional consensus — or shared consulting influence. Further source tracing is needed to distinguish the two. NLP similarity scoring across the corpus shows a coefficient of 0.74 for this specific phrase cluster.

Methodology note: Claims marked needs_review are excluded from aggregate counts. 47 claims in this section were flagged for conflicting source interpretations; they are counted in the base but noted where they affect percentages by more than 2 points.

Transparency Requirements

Disclosure requirements — telling audiences when AI was involved — are the second most common provision at 61%. But the definition of "disclosure" varies so widely that comparisons across policies are unreliable.2 The trend line is consistently upward — from 43% in 2022 to 61% today.

trend 2022–2026
Disclosure requirement prevalence has grown 18pp since 2022. See Figure 3.
Seven distinct definitions of "AI disclosure" identified across European policies. RISJ 2025.
"Fewer than a third of transparency provisions include any audit right or mechanism for verifying compliance." From the transparency analysis below

Some policies require disclosure of AI involvement in any published content; others only require it for AI-generated text; still others limit it to fully autonomous publication with no human review. The original draft of this article incorrectly conflated these categories. The practical implications differ substantially.

Vendor due diligence

Only 37% of reviewed policies address vendor relationships — the largest gap relative to the scale of the risk.

Organizations are outsourcing editorial AI decisions to vendors while retaining nominal editorial responsibility. This creates accountability gaps that disclosure requirements alone cannot close.

The three criteria that matter

Policies that do address vendors tend to converge on three provisions: training data disclosure, bias audit rights, and model change notification. Of these, only the last appears in a majority of vendor-aware policies.
